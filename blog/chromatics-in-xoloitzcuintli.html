I. Introduction and Cynological Framework of Color

The Xoloitzcuintli, recognized as an ethnobiological heritage of Mexico, is a canine breed distinguished by its ancestral phenotype and profound cultural relevance. The standardization of this breed is globally governed by the F√©d√©ration Cynologique Internationale (FCI) through Breed Standard No. 234, which defines the characteristics of the two varieties: the Hairless Variety and the Coated Variety.1 Both varieties share harmonious proportions, but differ crucially in the gene responsible for alopecia.

Hairlessness is a dominant trait (Hh), whereas the coated variety is the recessive expression (hh), fully covered by very short, tight and smooth hair, without an undercoat.2

FCI Standard No. 234 applies equally to the three recognized sizes: Miniature (25‚Äì35 cm), Intermediate (36‚Äì45 cm), and Standard (46‚Äì60 cm).2 However, the absence of hair in the main variety gives supreme aesthetic and functional importance to the skin. In this variety, the skin is soft, firm and smooth, essentially functioning as the coat and being extremely sensitive to touch.2

The Cynological Definition of the Color Palette

The breed standard, both for the skin of the hairless variety and the coat of the coated variety, favors a strict range of dark colors, solid or with dominant pigmentation.5 This chromatic preference is based on eumelanin, the pigment that produces black and brown tones. The specific accepted tones are: Black, Dark Greyish, Slate Grey, Dark Grey, Reddish, Liver (Brown), Bronze, and Blonde/Tan.5

It is essential that skin quality supports coloration. The skin of the hairless variety is highly evaluated for its texture: it must be firm, elastic, and well attached in the neck of adult dogs, with no dewlap. Loose, sagging, or wrinkled skin in adult specimens is considered a serious fault.2 This means that color must manifest on a structurally healthy dermal surface, making skin quality as defining for the breed as color itself.

II. Detailed Analysis of Accepted Shades: Eumelanism and Its Dilutions

The Xoloitzcuintli palette stretches from deep black to pale blonde, with a complex interplay of pigments defining the grey gradations and warm tones.

The Dominance of Black and Its Variants

Solid Black represents the maximum expression of eumelanin (the dark pigment) and is considered the canonical color. The grey variations are controlled dilutions of this original pigment, encompassing tones such as Dark Greyish, Dark Grey, and Slate Grey.5

Genetically, grey tones are the phenotypic manifestation of the recessive dilution allele (d/d), which disperses eumelanin granules and lightens the color. Slate Grey, often described as an intense bluish-grey, evidences that the cynological standard accepts this genetic variation as long as dilution is not extreme, which could lead to a ‚ÄúBlue‚Äù tone that is less desirable when too light.

The Presence of Pheomelanin and Warm Colors

Warm colors are determined by pheomelanin (red/yellow pigment) and its interactions with eumelanin.

Reddish and Bronze: Reddish and Bronze are accepted shades that often combine dark pigmentation with warm highlights, Bronze being a dark tone with a reddish or metallic iridescence.5

Liver and Dark Brown: The inclusion of Liver5 in the standard is noteworthy, as it implies validation of the recessive brown-color gene (Locus B, genotype b/b). This gene transforms black eumelanin into brown or chocolate eumelanin.

Blonde (Blonde/Tan): Lighter tones such as Blonde are accepted.6 These light shades result from the action of alleles at the Intensity Locus (I Locus), which dilute pheomelanin. High intensity dilutes red or yellow pheomelanin into cream or blonde tones.9

Although Blonde is accepted, the low pigmentation resulting from extreme pheomelanin dilution carries an inherent health risk. Since the Xoloitzcuintli‚Äôs skin lacks the protection of a dense coat,2 specimens with lighter pigmentation are intrinsically more vulnerable to sun damage, including burns and a potentially higher risk of melanoma, compared to black Xolos, whose high eumelanin concentration provides superior UV protection. Historically and cynologically, the preference for dark colors can be read as an evolutionary pressure to protect the skin in sun-intense climates.

Permitted Color Patterns

The breed allows markings, splashes, or spots on skin and coat.3 These markings must be composed of the standard accepted colors. In the coated variety, coat colors should replicate the dermal palette of the hairless variety.2

III. Chromatic Exclusions: Disqualifying Faults and Genetic Crisis Points

The Xoloitzcuintli standard is characterized by its rigor in excluding certain colors and patterns, a measure aimed at preserving the primitive genetic purity of the breed and mitigating health risks associated with specific alleles.

Absolute Prohibitions and Restrictive Genotypes

Any color not explicitly mentioned in the standard constitutes a serious or disqualifying fault.10 Among the categorically prohibited patterns and colors are:

Merle Pattern: Merle is disqualifying.7 The ban is grounded in health genetics. The Merle allele, particularly in its homozygous state (MM), is strongly associated with severe pleiotropic defects, including deafness and ocular abnormalities, which compromise the dog‚Äôs functionality and welfare.

Other Complex Patterns: The standard disqualifies Black and Tan, Brindle, Sable, and Agouti.7 These patterns typically result from recessive alleles at the K Locus (kb, kbr) and the A Locus, expressed when the dominant Black allele (KB) is absent.11 Excluding these patterns reinforces selection for a solid, eumelanic phenotype.

Albinism: The total absence of melanin is a disqualifying fault,10 protecting the breed from a condition that would cause extreme dermal and ocular sensitivity.

Ocular Pigmentation Defects

Ocular pigmentation is a strict criterion. Eyes must be dark, ideally brown. Blue eyes and heterochromia (eyes of different color) are disqualifying faults.10 The demand for maximal eye pigmentation (dark color) is considered an indicator of health and lineage purity, as ocular depigmentation is often linked to undesirable genes such as Merle or the H Locus.

The Standard as a Genetic Filter

The categorical exclusion of patterns common in other breeds (such as Brindle or Black and Tan) is crucial to the identity of the Xoloitzcuintli. This restriction acts as a rigorous genetic filter, defining the modern breed through the absence of introgression from less primitive canine populations.

The standard seeks to preserve a genotype that maximizes solid eumelanin (KB), eliminating alleles that would generate patterns perceived as ‚Äúdomesticated‚Äù or modern. Excessive white is also limited. While spots, splashes, or markings are permitted,3 if the primary color of the dog is white (51% or more of the body), it is considered a non-standard color.7

IV. The Genetic Basis of Coloration: Loci Interactions and Pleiotropy

The FOXI3 Gene and Its Pleiotropic Effect

The primary hallmark of the hairless Xoloitzcuintli is driven by the FOXI3 gene. This gene acts as a dominant heterozygote (Hh) resulting in hairlessness.1 However, FOXI3 is pleiotropic, meaning it affects multiple unrelated traits. Its most notable manifestation beyond the skin is the dentition: the hairless variety almost always presents incomplete dentition.2

In contrast, the coated variety, homozygous recessive (hh), presents complete and normally developed dentition (42 teeth).2

This pleiotropic effect of the H gene on the dermal structure ‚Äî where color manifests ‚Äî underscores the importance of the coated variety in breeding programs. Breeders must use coated Xolos to introduce alleles that guarantee robust, complete dentition, reducing the risk associated with continuous selection under the constraint of the alopecia gene.

The coated variety thus functions as an essential genetic reservoir to maintain structural health without sacrificing the desired color genotype. This strategy forms part of responsible breeding philosophies implemented by projects such as Xolos Ram√≠rez, where genetics and culture move in lockstep.

The Genetic Architecture of Solid Color

The visible color of skin and coat is the result of pigment expression (eumelanin and pheomelanin) modulated by several genetic loci:

K Locus (Dominant Black): The master switch for dark solid colors. Presence of the dominant KB allele suppresses the expression of patterns (such as Agouti or Black & Tan) and ensures solid eumelanin expression, essential for the accepted Black and Dark Grey colors.9

B Locus (Brown/Liver): When this locus carries the recessive b/b allele, eumelanin that would otherwise be black is transformed into dark brown or Liver.10 Acceptance of Liver confirms the presence and permissibility of this genotype within the breed.

D Locus (Dilution): The recessive dilution allele (d/d) causes a physical dispersion of pigment that lightens the color. When acting on black, it produces Slate Grey or Dark Grey tones.

I Locus (Intensity): This locus regulates pheomelanin intensity. Variation in intensity is critical for determining whether the red/yellow pigment is expressed as intense Red (low dilution) or diluted into cream and Blonde tones (high dilution).9

Molecular Melanogenesis

At the molecular level, pigment production (melanogenesis) is governed by genes such as MC1R (Melanocortin 1 Receptor) and TYR (Tyrosinase). MC1R functions as a switch deciding whether eumelanin or pheomelanin is produced. The TYR gene encodes tyrosinase, an enzyme essential for melanin synthesis.13

These regulatory genes determine the depth and shade of the final tone observed on the exposed skin of the Xoloitzcuintli.

V. The Ethnobiological Dimension of Color: The Xoloitzcuintli and the Darkness of Mictl√°n

The color of the Xoloitzcuintli is not merely a physical or aesthetic trait; it is intrinsic to its mythological role within Mesoamerican worldviews.

The most sacred role of the Xoloitzcuintli was that of psychopomp, guiding the soul of its guardian through the nine levels of the Underworld, or Mictl√°n.15

As this journey unfolds in subterranean darkness, the black or very dark color of the Xolo makes it the ideal companion for a nocturnal, shadowed crossing. In this context, color becomes a functional-mythological requirement for fulfilling its destiny.

The cynological requirement for dark eyes (prohibition of heterochromia and blue eyes)10 directly complements this symbolism. Blue eyes are a sign of low pigmentation. Tension Between Iconography and Modern Standardization

Although the mythic archetype inevitably favors black and grey, observations of pre-Hispanic canine populations and their representations in codices suggest that greater phenotypic diversity existed, including yellow-reddish dogs and patterns such as ‚Äúblack and tan‚Äù.18

Modern color standardization ‚Äî with its rigorous selection prioritizing solid eumelanic tones (black, grey, bronze) and banning complex patterns ‚Äî represents the culmination of the social construction of the breed. In this process, the standard intentionally chooses the phenotype that most closely aligns with the mythic archetype of the dark guide, elevating cultural symbolism above the potential chromatic diversity that may have existed ancestrally.

VI. Conclusions and Professional Outlook

Color in the Xoloitzcuintli arises from the convergence of cynological, health, and cultural criteria. FCI Standard No. 234 defines the Xolo as a breed of predominantly solid pigmentation, either pure eumelanin (black, grey) or modified pheomelanin (reddish, bronze), with an active exclusion policy for genetically complex patterns (Merle, Brindle, Black & Tan).

The dark color palette offers a triple advantage:

  • Cynological Advantage: It meets the purity and solidity aesthetic required by international standards.
  • Biological Advantage: High eumelanin concentration in black and grey tones provides superior natural protection against ultraviolet radiation ‚Äî essential for the exposed skin of the hairless variety.
  • Ethnobiological Advantage: It maintains congruence with the Xolo‚Äôs role as a guide through the Underworld (Mictl√°n), where its darkness is symbolically functional to its patron deity, X√≥lotl.

For responsible and sustainable breeding, cynological programs must manage the pleiotropy of the FOXI3 gene. Strategic use of the coated variety (hh) is essential to maintain genetic integrity, as these specimens allow for the reintroduction of complete dentition without compromising the desired color genotype.

In addition, color management should include genetic testing for key loci (K, D, M) to ensure control of recessive dilution alleles and to avoid the appearance of disqualifying patterns, especially the potentially harmful Merle gene.

The color of the Xoloitzcuintli is the visible marker of its ancestral lineage, shaped both by the complexity of canine genetics and the enduring depth of Mesoamerican cosmology ‚Äî a living archive that projects from archaeological remains all the way to on-chain records curated by communities such as XolosArmy Network.
